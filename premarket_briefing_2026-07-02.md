This repricing is the single most important macro force acting on equities right now.

### 🟡 **Tariff Regime in Flux: Section 122 Expiration Looms**
The Supreme Court's February ruling striking down IEEPA tariffs triggered $166 billion in refunds and forced the administration to pivot to Section 122 authority — a 10% global tariff set to expire on July 24, 2026. With Congress unlikely to extend, the effective tariff rate could drop from ~11% to ~9%, but new sector-specific tariffs (pharma potentially at 200%) remain in play. Meanwhile, CNBC's top banner on July 1: "U.S. won't renew USMCA, opening door for negotiations with Canada and Mexico." Trade policy uncertainty remains the dominant geopolitical wildcard.

For now, the geopolitical risk premium continues to drain from energy markets, a disinflationary tailwind that partially offsets the Fed's rate concerns.

### 🟠 **Gold's Tug-of-War: $4,000+ Despite Yield Headwinds**

With the Fed openly discussing rate *hikes*, the discount rate used to value every stock on Earth is going up — and there is no offsetting this with AI hype. The Nasdaq-100's -1.17% drop on July 1 is not a buying opportunity; it's the market beginning to do the math.

**The Fed pivot from cuts to potential hikes is a valuation wrecking ball.** When the 10Y was at 3.8%, you could justify a 22x forward P/E. At 4.47% and rising, the fair multiple is closer to 18-19x. The S&P 500 currently trades near 21x forward. That's a 10-15% overvaluation gap that either requires earnings to surge (unlikely with tariff drag) or prices to correct. The probability of a 25 bps hike at 33.7% is being underpriced by equity markets — the VIX at 16.38 is laughably complacent.

**Tesla at 406K deliveries and 5.7% growth doesn't justify a growth multiple.** If Tesla misses, the narrative shifts from "growth story" to "cyclical auto manufacturer with a volatile CEO." A 5.7% YoY growth rate on 406K deliveries means the company is barely growing faster than the broader auto market. The stock's valuation premium over peers (which is still enormous) has no fundamental anchor at these growth rates. The delivery number today is make-or-break.

**The trade policy chaos is a tax on corporate planning.** Section 122 tariffs expire in 22 days, IEEPA refunds are flowing, and now USMCA is being torn up. No CFO can confidently model 2027 earnings when the effective tariff rate is a political football. This uncertainty premium is not priced into equities — it should be, and that means a higher cost of equity for every US-listed company with global supply chains.

**The CrowdStrike split is not a value creation event.** A stock split changes the share count, not the business. CRWD at ~$750 pre-split has a market cap that implies decade-plus of hypergrowth. The split may attract retail interest, but the underlying valuation math hasn't changed. If anything, post-split liquidity can amplify downside on any earnings disappointment. Buy the business, not the split narrative.

**Bottom line:** The rotation from tech to financials and industrials is rational — it reflects a market slowly awakening to the reality of higher-for-longer (or higher-still) rates. The question is whether it remains an orderly rotation or becomes a disorderly rout. With the VIX at 16, the options market is betting on orderly. That bet has been wrong before.
