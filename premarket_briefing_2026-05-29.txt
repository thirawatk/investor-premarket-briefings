  3. KEY THEMES
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  **A. Yields Plunge on Growth Fears -- The Bond Market Is Talking**

  Treasury yields fell sharply across the curve Thursday, with the 2Y dropping
  12bps to 4.01% -- the biggest move on the curve. This "bull steepening"
  (short end falling faster than long end) signals the market is pricing in
  weaker growth and earlier Fed easing. The 10Y-2Y spread has now re-steepened
  to +49bps from +43bps. This is NOT a recession signal per se, but it IS the
  bond market saying that the soft GDP print (Q1 revised down to 1.6% annual,
  with some estimates as low as 0.4% quarterly) is forcing a re-think of the
  "no cuts in 2026" narrative.

  **B. Core PCE Lands at 3.3% -- Sticky Inflation Complicates the Fed's Path**

  Thursday's April core PCE came in at 3.3% YoY, exactly as expected. Headline
  PCE rose 0.4% MoM vs March's 0.7%. While the MoM deceleration is welcome,
  3.3% core is still well above the Fed's 2% target. The market now faces a
  tension: bonds are rallying on growth weakness, but inflation data does not
  give the Fed a clean mandate to ease. CME FedWatch shows ~70% probability
  of a hold at June 16-17, ~28% chance of a cut. The base case is "higher
  for even longer," and that itself is a risk to risk assets.

  **C. Iran War & Strait of Hormuz -- The Conflict That Won't Fully Resolve**

  The US-Iran ceasefire remains fragile. Iran's IRGC maintains de facto control
  of the Strait of Hormuz, and the US Navy has been conducting escort
  operations to reopen shipping lanes. The US has sanctioned the "Persian Gulf
  Strait Authority" for extorting tolls. Most maritime traffic through the
  Strait has been halted or severely disrupted since February. WTI crude at
  $89 continues to embed a "Hormuz risk premium" of perhaps $15-20/barrel.
  Any further escalation could spike oil to $100+, which would be a direct
  shock to inflation expectations and consumer spending.

  **D. Nvidia Earnings -- AI Euphoria Cooling Into Discipline**

  Nvidia's earnings (reported May 21) beat estimates but the stock fell
  post-earnings, suggesting the bar had been set impossibly high. The
  "AI trade" is now selectively rotating: memory stocks (Micron, SK Hynix)
  are rallying on HBM demand, while Nvidia itself faces concerns about
  export restrictions and China demand. For the broader market, the key
  question is whether AI capex can sustain earnings growth across the S&P
  500 or whether it remains a narrow trade concentrated in a handful of
  mega-caps. The Nasdaq underperformance in pre-market (-0.15%) is a small
  tell.

  **E. The Dollar Is Stuck in No Man's Land**

  The DXY has been consolidating around the 98.0-98.50 range, caught between
  two opposing forces: safe-haven demand from the Iran war pulling it up,
  and rate-cut expectations pushing it down. EUR/USD at 1.1617 is relatively
  stable, and USD/JPY at ~159 suggests the BoJ is in no hurry to intervene
  again unless the pace of yen weakness accelerates. The dollar's direction
  will likely be determined by the June FOMC meeting outcome -- a hawkish
  hold sends DXY higher, a dovish pivot sends it lower.

  **F. ISM Services and the "Last Big Data Point" Before May ISM Manufacturing**

  Today's ISM Services PMI (the report covers May) is one of the last major
  data points before the June 16-17 FOMC meeting. The services sector has been
  the economy's growth engine, and any significant softening here would
  accelerate rate-cut expectations. Analysts will watch the prices-paid
  sub-index closely, as it feeds directly into the Fed's inflation dashboard.
  Regional Fed data has shown prices-paid averaging 54.9, and PPI final
  demand is already running at 6.0% YoY -- the inflation pipeline is NOT clean.

  **G. Consumer Sentiment Remains Depressed**

  The University of Michigan's consumer sentiment index started 2026 at
  historically low levels (~54), reflecting anxiety about inflation, geopolitics,
  and the housing market. Existing home sales fell 1.9% in April to their
  worst level since 2009, with inventory surging 21% YoY. Mortgage rates near
  7% continue to lock in the "golden handcuffs" effect for homeowners. Weak
  housing + weak sentiment + tight credit = a consumer spending slowdown that
  hasn't fully shown up in the data yet but is coming.

  5. THE HARD TRUTH -- DAMODARAN LENS
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  Let's stop dancing around it. Here is what the data is actually telling you:

  **The market is priced for a soft landing that is NOT in the data.**

  The S&P 500 is trading near all-time highs. The VIX is at 15.74, near its
  lowest levels of the year. And yet: Q1 GDP growth has been revised DOWN to
  1.6% (annualized), core inflation is stuck at 3.3% (well above target),
  the consumer is depressed, housing is in the worst shape since 2009, and
  there is an active war constraining 20% of global oil transit. Where,
  exactly, is the "soft landing" priced into equities?

  **The bond market knows something the stock market won't admit.**

  The 2Y Treasury dropping 12bps in a single session is not noise. It is a
  sharp, deliberate repositioning by the deepest-pocketed players in global
  finance. They are betting on growth deterioration. If they are right,
  equities have a reckoning coming -- earnings estimates for H2 2026 will
  need to be cut, and the multiple will compress. The bull steepening of the
  yield curve has historically been a LEADING indicator of Fed cuts, and Fed
  cuts have historically been a LEADING indicator of recession.

  **The Fed is in a box, and June 17th is too far away.**

  With core PCE at 3.3%, the Fed cannot credibly cut rates without looking
  like they've abandoned their inflation mandate. But with growth decelerating
  and the labor market showing cracks (jobless claims ticking up to 215K),
  holding rates at 3.50-3.75% risks overtightening. The market is pricing
  a June hold, but the DISCONNECT between what bonds are saying (cut) and
  what inflation data says (can't cut) is a powder keg for volatility.

  **Opportunity cost of sitting in "complacent" allocations.**

  If you are 100% long equities and riding the AI wave, ask yourself: what
  is your downside protection if the Hormuz situation escalates, if oil
  spikes to $100+, or if June FOMC delivers a hawkish hold that punctures
  rate-cut expectations? Cash is yielding 3.68% (3-month T-bill). Short-duration
  bonds are paying 4%+ with minimal duration risk. The risk-adjusted return
  of sitting in the front end of the Treasury curve is genuinely competitive
  with equities at these valuations -- and you have dry powder for when the
  correction comes.

  **This is not a time for conviction. This is a time for optionality.**
