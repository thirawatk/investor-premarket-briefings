## Section 1: The Week in One Paragraph

This was a week of extraordinary, contradictory forces colliding. A **14-point US-Iran peace memorandum** was signed, ending the military conflict, reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and crushing WTI crude from $100+ war highs to $74 — a 30% plunge in under a month. The S&P 500 netted a mere +1.0% for the week — a flat finish that masks two of the most violently opposing forces the market has seen in 2026.

The **Bank of Japan** also joined the action, hiking rates to 1.0% — a 31-year high — yet the yen failed to rally (USD/JPY at 161), exposing the limits of BOJ policy against a hawkish Fed.

And the week ended with a record $6T+ triple witching options expiration executed flawlessly, plus a Juneteenth holiday closure on Friday.

## Section 8: Hard Truth — Damodaran Lens (Weekly Edition)

### What did the market get wrong this week?

The market priced the Iran peace deal and the hawkish FOMC as if they are independent events. **They are not.** The same forces that drove oil prices to $100+ (war, supply disruption) are what forced the Fed to hold rates steady rather than cut. Now that peace is here, oil is collapsing — which should *disinflate* the economy — yet the Fed *raised* its inflation projections and *signaled a hike*. This is not a Goldilocks scenario. It's a regime where the market expects lower inflation AND higher rates simultaneously. Historically, that combination lasts about as long as it takes for one of those legs to break.

### Where is the margin of safety thinner than Monday?

**Thinner everywhere.** The S&P 500 at 7,504 with the 10-year at 4.44% and the Fed signaling hikes means the equity risk premium is approximately zero. At 22x forward earnings ($340 EPS consensus), you are being paid essentially nothing over risk-free Treasuries to own the most uncertain macro environment in years. The Shiller CAPE is north of 35. Market cap-to-GDP is near all-time highs. This is not a cheap market by any value framework.

**The small-cap rotation everyone is celebrating is tactical, not structural.** Russell 2000 companies have lower margins, higher debt loads, and more rate sensitivity than the S&P 500. If the Fed actually hikes, small-caps get crushed. If the economy slows, small-cap earnings get hit first. The rotation into small-caps works only in a perfect Goldilocks scenario — peace, stable rates, moderate growth. You're betting on all three simultaneously.

### What divergence is building between price and fundamentals?

1. **Credit vs. Equities**: IG spreads at 75bp are pricing no recession, yet the yield curve (2s10s at +28bp) has only partially normalized from inversion. If a recession is not coming, why are long-term rates still so low relative to short rates?

2. **Gold vs. VIX**: Gold at $4,234 — still up 23% YoY — is screaming that structural instability in the monetary system persists. The VIX at 16.65 is pretending the world is calm. One of them is wrong. Historically, gold is the honest signal.

3. **Hawkish Fed vs. Falling Oil**: The bond market is pricing lower inflation (10Y down to 4.44%) while the Fed is pricing higher inflation (core PCE raised to 3.3%). These cannot both be right. Either the bond market is about to get a wake-up call from a sticky-CPI print, or the Fed will be forced to reverse its hawkish stance by year-end. The resolution of this divergence will define Q3.

4. **SpaceX at $2.75T**: A company with four trading days as a public entity is now the third most valuable company in the world. The last time markets priced something this quickly, it was... never. Because no IPO has been this large. The SeekingAlpha analysts warning about "IPO valuation risks" are the sober second thought you ignore at your peril.

### The 1 question no one asked this week that you should be asking:

**"If the US-Iran peace deal is so bullish for the global economy, why did gold only fall 3.4%, and why is it still at $4,234?"**

Gold didn't crash. It dipped. A genuine world-peace event in the Middle East — the kind of tail-risk removal that should send gold down 10-15% — barely dented the gold bull market. That tells you the gold rally is not about the Iran war. It's about something deeper: loss of faith in fiat currency management, central bank reserve diversification away from USD, and the structural erosion of purchasing power that no peace deal can fix.

If you're long equities and short gold, you're betting the market's interpretation of the peace deal is right and gold's is wrong. I'd ask yourself: which market has a better track record of pricing structural risk correctly?
