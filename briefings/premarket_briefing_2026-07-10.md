Markets are pricing a persistent geopolitical risk premium into energy and defense names while trimming exposure to airlines and consumer discretionary.

### 📊 **Stagflation Signal: Hot PPI + Weak Jobs = Fed Dilemma**
June PPI came in at +0.9% MoM — triple the +0.3% consensus and the largest monthly jump in over three years — while June nonfarm payrolls added just 57,000 jobs against expectations of 110,000–115,000. This combination of **hot producer prices and softening labor** is the textbook stagflationary setup the Fed most fears. Core PPI (ex-food, energy, trade) had already hit +0.8% in May, suggesting pipeline inflation is accelerating, not cooling. May headline CPI ran at 4.17% YoY — well above the Fed's 2% target — and the PPI print suggests consumer prices may not moderate quickly. The Fed is trapped: cut to support jobs and inflation could reignite; hike to fight inflation and the labor market cracks further.

### 🏛️ **FOMC Minutes: A Fed at War With Itself**
The July 8 release of the June 17 FOMC minutes confirmed a deeply divided committee. CME FedWatch assigns a 74.9% probability the Fed holds at the July 29 meeting, but markets now price a 64–76% chance of at least one hike by year-end. The "family fight" narrative has rattled bond markets: the 10Y yield hit 4.59%, its highest level since Warsh took the chair.

## 5. The Hard Truth — Damodaran Lens 🔍

Let's be brutally honest about where we stand:

**The market is pricing two incompatible scenarios simultaneously.** On one hand, the AI/tech complex trades as if we're in a secular growth story with rates heading lower — Nasdaq +21% YTD, P/E expansion across mega-caps, and retail piling into Motley Fool's "top 12 stocks" which are essentially the same 7 stocks everyone has owned for 18 months. On the other hand, the bond market, commodities, and the Fed's own projections are screaming that inflation is _not_ dead, the Fed is _not_ cutting, and the geopolitical risk premium is _not_ zero.

**The June PPI at +0.9% is the number that should terrify you.** Not because one hot PPI print breaks the economy — but because it confirms what value investors have been warning about since early 2026: the "last mile" of inflation is not just sticky, it's reversing. Core PPI ran +0.8% in May and now headline PPI at +0.9% in June. These are not transitory — they're the pipeline telling you CPI is going to stay above 3.5–4.0% for the rest of 2026. At a 3.50% fed funds rate, that means **real rates are still negative**. Historically, when real rates are negative and inflation is accelerating, P/E multiples contract — not expand.

**The Fed's "family fight" is not a curiosity — it's a warning.** When 9 of 18 FOMC members want to hike while the economy just added 57K jobs, you have a committee that's prioritizing inflation credibility over growth. Warsh's explicit call for shrinking the balance sheet by selling longer-dated bonds is the most hawkish balance-sheet signal since Quantitative Tightening began. If he gets his way, the 10Y could easily break above 5.0%, and at that level, every DCF model with a terminal growth rate above 3% needs to be re-run.

**PepsiCo's results are the canary.** Revenue beat, EPS missed. That's margin compression from input costs — the exact dynamic that PPI is warning about. If PepsiCo, with its pricing power and global scale, can't protect margins, what happens to the hundreds of mid-cap consumer names that lack that power? Q2 earnings season is going to be a margin stress test, not a growth story.

**On Iran**: Markets are treating this as a transitory shock — VIX at 16.79 is not pricing in a sustained conflict. But the Strait of Hormuz carries 20% of global oil supply. If this escalates to a blockade scenario, the oil spike isn't $10 — it's $30–50. And unlike 2022, the Fed can't "look through" an energy shock this time because inflation is already above target. The asymmetry is stark: limited upside if tensions cool (already partially priced), catastrophic downside if they escalate.

**The value investor's posture**: Cash optionality has value right now. The equity risk premium — the excess return stocks offer over risk-free bonds — is compressed to levels that only make sense if you believe both (a) earnings will grow at above-trend rates and (b) the risk-free rate will fall. Both assumptions are under direct assault from the data. The S&P 500 at 7,500 and a 4.59% 10Y is a bet that everything goes right. That's not a bet value investors should be making with full conviction.
