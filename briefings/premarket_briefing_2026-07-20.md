## 🔑 Key Themes

The PHLX Semiconductor Index (SOX) has entered a technical correction, falling over 13% in the past month — though notably still up ~63% year-to-date. The triggers were threefold: (1) TSMC's Q2 earnings and outlook disappointed on AI chip demand trajectory; (2) Netflix's subscriber growth warning raised questions about monetization timelines for AI investments; (3) Chinese start-up Moonshot AI released a model competitive with US leaders, threatening to undercut pricing. SanDisk (-24.6%), Marvell (-17.4%), and Western Digital (-13.6%) were the week's casualties. The Barron's noted the SOX "briefly traded in the green" on Friday — suggesting dip-buyers are lurking.

### 2. 🛢️ US-Iran War: Strait of Hormuz = Global Oil Shock
Oil prices surged 12% in a single week to $82.26/bbl, the sharpest weekly move of 2026, as the US-Iran military conflict escalated dramatically. The Strait of Hormuz — conduit for ~25% of global seaborne oil — has been effectively blocked by Iran since February 28. Trump's new proposal for a 20% transit fee on Hormuz shipping added fuel to the supply panic. The IEA has now characterized this as the "largest supply disruption" in the market. OPEC cut its demand forecast again, but the market has started "looking past Hormuz" — implying some level of structural adaptation. Energy was the only positive S&P 500 sector on Friday. CSIS warns the conflict "continues to reshape the Middle East," with F1 Grand Prix races cancelled and the economic impact now meriting its own Wikipedia page.

**The semiconductor sell-off is not a crash — it's gravity.** A sector that ran +63% YTD on the premise of infinite AI capex is now being forced to answer the only question that matters in valuation: *what are the cash flows, and when do they arrive?* TSMC's disappointing outlook and Moonshot AI's low-cost Chinese model are not anomalies — they are the first cracks in the assumption that US firms can charge monopoly rents for AI infrastructure forever. Damodaran would remind us that every gold rush ends the same way: the shovel sellers get rich first, then competition destroys margins. The SOX at 11,674 is still pricing in terminal growth rates that require AI to become the largest profit pool in human history. Be honest: is that the base case, or the bull case?

**Oil at $82 is not a supply story — it's an option value on war.** The Strait of Hormuz crisis has added a geopolitical risk premium of roughly $15-20/bbl to crude. Damodaran would strip this out: the normalized WTI price absent war fears is closer to $60-65. If you're buying energy stocks today, you're not buying cash flows — you're buying an undiversifiable bet on Middle East escalation. That's not investing; that's speculating with a macro tailwind.

**The FOMC is trapped.** Cooler CPI says "hold or cut." War-driven oil says "hike to preempt supply-side inflation." Damodaran's framework would note that neither is a reason to change your equity risk premium — the ERP should reflect genuine uncertainty, not policy noise. At 4.55% on the 10Y and 7,458 on the S&P, the implied ERP is roughly 4.0-4.5% — slightly below the long-run average of ~4.5-5.0%. The market is not cheap. It's fairly priced for a soft landing that may not arrive.

**This week's earnings are make-or-break for the Mag 7 multiple.** Tesla, Alphabet, and ServiceNow command rich multiples because the market believes their AI narratives. If any of them report anything resembling IBM's "customers are shifting spend away from us" moment, the rotation from growth to value could accelerate violently. The SOX's intraday recovery from -5.7% to -1.6% on Friday suggests dip-buyers are still active — but they're fighting the Fed, geopolitics, and gravity simultaneously. The hard truth: the easy money in 2026 was made in H1. H2 is about survival.
